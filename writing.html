The Lake Remembers Winter

On why Chicago’s May keeps running cold — and what you learn about it from the deck of a boat

By the first of May, the calendar has technically settled the matter. Spring is here. The rest of the country — or at least, the parts that don’t share a shoreline with a body of water the size of a small sea — is already beginning to act accordingly. Tulips are out. People have located their linen. And then you step off the gangway at Navy Pier at eleven in the morning, the wind coming east off Lake Michigan at eighteen knots, the temperature somewhere in the low forties, and the calendar can do whatever it likes.

“Lake Michigan holds roughly 1,180 cubic miles of water. That is not something that warms up because the calendar turned a page.”

Chicago’s spring has always been a negotiation, but May specifically has become something of a running joke — or would be, if the punch line weren’t so consistent. A few warm days arrive in late April; they raise the city’s hopes; something resets. The winter coat goes back into the closet. You get it back out. You start carrying it in a bag because you have stopped trusting the forecast. This is the life.

The proximate culprit is the lake. Lake Michigan holds roughly 1,180 cubic miles of water, and by the time April ends, most of that water is still somewhere between 38 and 48 degrees Fahrenheit — it has been absorbing heat since March, but slowly, because it is a very large thermal mass and physics does not care about your optimism. When the wind comes off the water, as it often does on the south end of a north-south oriented lake, it carries that cold into the city. This is the spring counterpart to the winter lake effect — not the kind that drops two feet of snow on Buffalo, but the quieter kind that simply suppresses temperatures on shoreline days when you’d otherwise have room to breathe. The National Weather Service calls the resulting spread between inland and lakeside temperatures a “lake breeze gradient.” Everyone else calls it May.

The lake thermal lag is structural and it is not getting better. Warmer winters have meant reduced ice coverage — which sounds like a reprieve but is not. Ice acts as an insulating cap that slows the lake’s late-winter cooling. Without it, the water enters spring already cold all the way down, and the spring warmup is consequently slower, not faster. The surface may look different; the column does not. Climatologists at the University of Michigan and NOAA’s Great Lakes Environmental Research Laboratory have documented this pattern in the observational record, and the projections do not suggest it will self-correct.

Then there is the jet stream, which has been misbehaving. The polar vortex — once a reliable fixture of winter and a mostly irrelevant abstraction by May — has been less predictable over the last decade. As the Arctic warms faster than lower latitudes, the temperature gradient that keeps the polar jet stream tightly circling the pole weakens. A weakened jet meanders. It dips south. When it dips south over the Great Lakes region in May, it pulls in air that has no business being this far south at this time of year. Whether this is a trend or an extended run of variance is a live question in the atmospheric science literature. The lived experience — cold Mays with occasional taunting warm days — is not imaginary.

I have now spent two May seasons working the lake on a hundred-foot tour boat, and there is a moment that repeats several times per shift without fail. A family boards from the gangway in boat shoes and shorts, takes two steps onto the open deck, looks at the skyline, looks at the lake, looks at me standing there in my company fleece, and recalibrates. Sometimes they go back down to buy a blanket from the gift shop. Sometimes they don’t, and they spend the next ninety minutes quietly suffering in the name of the aesthetic. I have given the tour speech three hundred times and I still feel a small, private satisfaction when someone from Dallas discovers that the lake is its own weather system. It does not care about your arrival, your expectations, or the fact that this is supposed to be a summer boat tour. It has been here longer than any of us, and it has a longer memory for winter than we do. That is the whole story, in miniature. Chicago in May is not broken. It is just downstream of something very large and very cold, and those conditions change on their own schedule, not on yours.

On the Causes of Seasickness

Seasickness is not an illness — it is a complaint filed by the brain when the inner ear, the eyes, and the body disagree about what the world is doing. The most provocative condition is slow vertical and rotary motion with no visual horizon: the inside of a cabin on a rocky lake michigan day, for instance. The vestibular system bears most of the responsibility; people with absent vestibular function do not get seasick, which is one of the more elegant natural experiments available to the field. Susceptibility varies by age, sex, and sleep deprivation — there is a hereditary component — and experienced sailors adapt over days to months of repeated exposure. Symptoms progress predictably: malaise and yawning before nausea, nausea before vomiting, sweating and pallor throughout. Non-pharmaceutical treatment reduces to one principle: minimize the conflict. Seek the horizon, stand where the boat moves least, let fresh air in, wait. Drugs help, but placebo accounts for ten to forty percent of apparent drug benefit — which is another way of saying that confidence and expectation are themselves a mechanism, and that acting like you know what you are doing is not entirely theatrical (in this regard).
