The Lake Remembers Winter

On why Chicago’s May keeps running cold

By the first of May, the calendar has technically settled the matter. Spring is here. The rest of the country — or at least, the parts that don’t share a shoreline with a body of water the size of a small sea — is already beginning to act accordingly. Tulips are out. People have located their linen. Stepping onto Navy Pier at eight in the morning, with the wind coming east off Lake Michigan at eighteen knots and the temperature anywhere from forty to sixty, truly demonstrates that our Chicago ecosystem can do whatever it likes.

“Lake Michigan holds roughly 1,180 cubic miles of water. That is not something that warms up because the calendar turned a page. The constant operation of a Nuclear Power plant for eleven and a quarter days would produce approximately enough energy to warm the lake by a single degree farenheit.”

Chicago's spring has always been a negotiation, but May specifically has become something of a running joke — or would be, if the punch line weren't so consistent. A few warm days arrive in late April raising everyone's hopes. The winter coat goes back into the closet. You get it back out. You start carrying it in a bag because you have stopped trusting the forecast.

The proximate culprit is Lake Michigan, holding roughly 1,180 cubic miles of water. By the time April ends, most of that water is still somewhere between 38 and 48 degrees Fahrenheit — it has been absorbing heat since March, but slowly. The immense thermal capacity does not care about your optimism. When the wind comes off the water, it carries that cold into the city. This is the spring counterpart to the winter lake effect — not the kind that drops two feet of snow on Buffalo, but the quieter kind that simply suppresses temperatures on shoreline days. The National Weather Service calls the resulting spread between inland and lakeside temperatures a “lake breeze gradient.” Everyone here calls it May.

The lake thermal lag is structural and it is not getting better. Warmer winters have meant reduced ice coverage — which sounds like a reprieve but is not. Ice acts as an insulating cap that slows the lake's late-winter cooling. Without it, the water enters spring already cold all the way down, and the spring warmup is slower, not faster. The surface may look different; the column does not. Climatologists at the University of Michigan and NOAA's Great Lakes Environmental Research Laboratory have documented this pattern in the observational record, and the projections do not suggest it will self-correct.

Then there is the jet stream, which has been misbehaving. The polar vortex — once a reliable fixture of winter and a mostly irrelevant abstraction by May — has been less predictable over the last decade. As the Arctic warms faster than lower latitudes, the temperature gradient that keeps the polar jet stream tightly circling the pole weakens. When it dips south over the Great Lakes region in May, it pulls in air that has no business being this far south at this time of year. Whether this is a trend or an extended run of variance is a live question in the atmospheric science literature. The lived experience — cold Mays with occasional taunting warm days — is not imaginary.

This was my fourth May season working the lake on the Spirit of Navy Pier, and I also often fall victim to the optimism of a warm day. Stepping outside my Bridgeport apartment has never been a true indicator of what the day will be like on the water. The lake has been here longer than any of us, and it has a longer memory for winter than we do. Chicago in May is not broken - just frustratingly subject to the Lake, the Polar Vortex, and the jet stream. It is a reminder that those conditions change on their own schedule, not on ours.

How Many Variables Describe a Protein, Really?

Proteins can comprise thousands of atoms, yet are typically folded into a small number of three-dimensional conformations. The coordinate space is enormous; the space of behaviours, mercifully, is not.

"Biologically meaningful dynamics often reside on low-dimensional manifolds, even when embedded in an extremely high-dimensional coordinate space."

The classical answer is dimensionality reduction: principal components, time-lagged components, diffusion maps, machine learning, deep neural-nets. Each works, each has costs. What I have argued is that the right number of variables is not a property of the protein but of the question, and that variational autoencoders are one of the few tools that lets you ask both at once.

On the Causes of Seasickness

Seasickness is not an illness — it is a complaint filed by the brain when the inner ear, the eyes, and the body disagree about what the world is doing. The most provocative condition is slow vertical and rotary motion with no visual horizon: the inside of a cabin on a rocky lake michigan day, for instance. The vestibular system bears most of the responsibility; people with absent vestibular function do not get seasick, which is one of the more elegant natural experiments available to the field. Susceptibility varies by age, sex, and sleep deprivation — there is a hereditary component — and experienced sailors adapt over days to months of repeated exposure. Symptoms progress predictably: malaise and yawning before nausea, nausea before vomiting, sweating and pallor throughout. Non-pharmaceutical treatment reduces to one principle: minimize the conflict. Seek the horizon, stand where the boat moves least, let fresh air in, wait. Drugs help, but placebo accounts for ten to forty percent of apparent drug benefit — which is another way of saying that confidence and expectation are themselves a mechanism, and that acting like you know what you are doing is not entirely theatrical (in this regard).
